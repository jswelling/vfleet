Ray Casting

Ray casting is a backward rendering method.

  • For each screen pixel, a ray is shot into the sample volume. Color and opacity are numerically integrated along the ray, until it leaves the volume or reaches an opacity of 1.0. The resulting opacity and color are assigned to the pixel.

    Accuracy of numerical integration is one factor affecting rendering time and image accuracy.

    • Ideally, you can step along the ray by small increments, using the trilenearly interpolated color and opacity at each sample point.
    • Alternately, take bigger steps and assume color and opacity are constant within a voxel.
  • In general, one ray per pixel is enough (unlike ray tracing of surface data).
  • Ray casting is accurate but slow. Tricks to speed it up:
    • Skip empty or constant parts of the volume.
    • Stop before opacity reaches 1.0.
    • Adaptive sampling of screen.
  • Shear-Warp algorithms are a way to accelerate ray casting by shearing the volume so the ray stepping algorithm is simple, then warping the resulting image to correct for the effects of the shear.
